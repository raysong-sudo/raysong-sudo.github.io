Scoliosis

Your curve. Not the textbook's.

Scoliosis isn't one condition, it's your spine, your curve pattern, your Cobb angle, your age. The Schroth Method is the only approach built for that specificity. Every case here is led by a Schroth-certified clinician.

The condition

A curve measured in degrees. A body that has adapted around it for years.

Scoliosis is a lateral curvature of the spine, usually also involving rotation of the vertebrae and ribcage. It's measured in Cobb angle degrees on an X-ray. Curves under 25 degrees are typically monitored. Curves between 25 and 45 degrees are often managed with bracing in adolescents and exercise in adults. Curves above 45 degrees may require surgical consultation.

What the Cobb angle doesn't capture is how the body has adapted. Over years, the muscles, ribcage, and breathing mechanics all reorganize around the curve. Generic PT treats the number, the Schroth Method treats the pattern. Three-dimensional correction means breath, posture, and movement are trained together, not separately.

Who this is for

  • Adults with curve-related pain. Pain that's gotten worse, not better, with age. 02

The Schroth Method

Three-dimensional correction. Not adapted from general PT, built for curved spines.

The Schroth Method uses specific breathing patterns, postural corrections, and exercises designed for each patient's unique curve pattern. Sessions are led by a Schroth-certified clinician trained through the Hunter College program. Most patients complete 8 to 12 sessions. Adolescents in active growth may need ongoing support, especially if working alongside a brace program.

Questions

  • What's a Cobb angle and what does mine mean?

    The Cobb angle is the standard measurement of scoliosis severity on an X-ray, it measures the angle between the most tilted vertebrae at the top and bottom of the curve. Under 10 degrees is considered normal spinal variation. 10 to 25 degrees is mild scoliosis (monitored). 25 to 45 degrees is moderate (bracing considered for adolescents; exercise for adults). Above 45 degrees, surgical consultation is typically recommended. The Cobb angle matters for management decisions, but it doesn't fully predict pain or function.

  • Can Schroth PT reduce my Cobb angle?

    In adolescents whose spines are still growing, Schroth can slow progression and, in some cases, reduce the measured curve. In adults, structural reduction is less common, but pain reduction, improved posture, and stabilization of the curve are realistic goals. The more important question is whether the treatment improves how you feel and function day to day.

  • My surgeon said to wait and watch. Should I still do PT?

    Watching and waiting addresses the surgical threshold, not the lived experience of having scoliosis. PT can run concurrently with observation, it won't interfere with monitoring and typically helps with pain, posture, and confidence in movement. We coordinate with surgeons when helpful.

  • My child is 13 and just diagnosed. What should we do?

    Start sooner rather than later. Adolescents in active growth respond best to Schroth PT, especially if the Cobb angle is above 20 degrees or increasing. Early treatment can slow progression and establish the breathing and postural habits that carry forward into adulthood.
