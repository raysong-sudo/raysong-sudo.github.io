Pelvic pain (from endometriosis, interstitial cystitis, vulvodynia, or unknown origin) is undertreated and under-addressed in most clinical settings. We take it seriously. Dr. Jang leads every case.

Pelvic pain

The pelvic floor is a muscle group. Like any muscle group, it can be in spasm, weak, or uncoordinated.

Pelvic pain has many drivers: hypertonic (too tight) pelvic floor muscles, nerve sensitization, connective tissue restrictions, and in some cases underlying conditions like endometriosis or interstitial cystitis. The pain is real. The mechanism is usually musculoskeletal, even when the origin is gynecological or urological. That's where PT comes in.

Most patients with pelvic pain have already seen a gynecologist, urologist, or colorectal surgeon. What they haven't had is a thorough musculoskeletal assessment of the pelvic floor itself. Dr. Jang's approach starts there, evaluating the floor externally, assessing breath and hip mechanics, and only proceeding to internal assessment when it's clinically indicated and the patient is comfortable.

Conditions we treat

  • Vulvodynia and vestibulodynia. Pain with touch or penetration. ,
  • Endometriosis-related pelvic floor dysfunction. The muscle layer above the pathology. ,
  • Interstitial cystitis and bladder pain. When PT addresses the muscular component. ,
  • Levator ani syndrome and pelvic floor spasm. Persistent rectal or perineal pain. ,
  • Painful sex (dyspareunia). Treated directly, not worked around. ,

How we treat it

Down-training a hypertonic floor is as important as strengthening a weak one. Not every pelvic problem is a Kegel problem.

The treatment for pelvic pain is often the opposite of what patients expect. For a hypertonic (too tight) pelvic floor, the goal is relaxation and release, not strengthening. Dr. Jang uses manual therapy, breathing techniques, and graduated desensitization approaches. Internal treatment is offered when it's the right clinical choice and always with explicit informed consent. Patients move at the pace that feels safe and productive.

Questions

  • I've been told nothing is structurally wrong. Can PT still help?

    Yes, often significantly. Pelvic pain frequently persists after structural causes have been ruled out because the musculoskeletal layer (the pelvic floor muscles, connective tissue, and nerve pathways) has become sensitized or dysregulated independently of the original trigger. PT addresses exactly this layer.

  • Is internal examination required?

    No. Internal examination is offered when it's clinically useful and only proceeds with explicit informed consent. Many patients benefit substantially from external work, breathing techniques, and guided home exercises without any internal component. Dr. Jang will discuss the options and let you decide.

  • I have endometriosis. How does PT fit in?

    Endometriosis causes inflammation and adhesions that the pelvic floor muscles respond to over time, often by becoming hypertonic (too tight) as a protective mechanism. PT doesn't treat the endometriosis itself but can significantly reduce the muscular component of pain. Most endo patients who do PT alongside their gynecological management report better overall pain control.

  • How many sessions will I need?

    Pelvic pain is variable. Some patients with a single clear presentation (e.g., vaginismus with no other complicating factors) resolve in 6 to 8 sessions. Complex or long-standing conditions (endometriosis-related floor dysfunction, interstitial cystitis) may take 12 to 16 sessions and benefit from ongoing maintenance. Dr. Jang gives a realistic estimate at the end of the first evaluation.
