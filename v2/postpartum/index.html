Postpartum PT

Your body after. Treated specifically.

Pelvic floor rehabilitation, diastasis recti assessment, and return-to-activity planning, in the fourth trimester and beyond.

The fourth trimester

Delivery is an event. Recovery is a process.

The postpartum period brings a specific set of physical changes: pelvic floor trauma or weakening from delivery, abdominal muscle separation (diastasis recti), core instability, and hormonal changes that affect joint laxity and tissue healing. Standard obstetric care addresses these incompletely, a 6-week clearance does not mean the body is ready to return to exercise, running, or lifting.

Dr. Jang's postpartum evaluation looks at pelvic floor strength and coordination, diastasis recti severity and function, breathing mechanics, hip and SI joint stability, and readiness for progressive loading. The goal is not just clearance, it's confident return to whatever "normal activity" means for you, whether that's chasing a toddler, returning to running, or getting back to the gym.

Timing

You don't have to wait six weeks. You just have to wait until you're ready.

For vaginal delivery without significant tearing, gentle pelvic floor assessment can begin as early as 3 to 4 weeks postpartum. For cesarean delivery or significant perineal repair, 6 weeks is a reasonable starting point for an initial evaluation, though treatment intensity is calibrated to healing stage. The 6-week OB clearance is a standard milestone, not a signal that recovery is complete. Most patients benefit from PT that begins within the first 2 to 3 months and continues for 6 to 10 sessions.

Questions

  • I had a C-section. Does PT still apply?

    Yes. Cesarean delivery bypasses vaginal trauma but doesn't prevent pelvic floor dysfunction, pregnancy itself affects the pelvic floor regardless of delivery mode. C-section also creates scar tissue at the incision site that can affect core function, hip mobility, and sensation. Dr. Jang addresses all of this.

  • I'm six months postpartum. Is it too late?

    No. The body continues to respond to pelvic floor well beyond the immediate postpartum window. Patients 1, 2, or 5 years postpartum often find meaningful improvement. The "fourth trimester" framing shouldn't suggest that PT only belongs in the first few months.

  • I'm leaking when I run. Is that normal?

    It's common, but it's not something you have to accept. Stress urinary incontinence during running, jumping, or lifting is a sign of pelvic floor dysfunction that responds well to PT. Most patients resolve it within 6 to 8 sessions when the underlying coordination and strength deficit is addressed.

  • Do I need a referral from my OB?

    No. You can book directly. If your OB has already given 6-week clearance and you want to start PT, there's nothing else needed. If you'd like Dr. Jang to coordinate with your care team, she can send a progress note.
