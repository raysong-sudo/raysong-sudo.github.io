The method

Trained hands first. Movement follows.

Manual therapy is direct, hands-on work on the structures producing your pain. Myofascial release for soft tissue. Joint mobilization for stiff joints. Trigger point work for the muscle that has been guarding for years. Neuromuscular re-education for the movement pattern that keeps the pain coming back. Each technique is chosen and sequenced in real time, based on what the body is showing in that session.

Imagine the familiar relief of expert hands on tissue, paired with a clinical assessment that identifies where to work and why. Movement and load work follow the hands-on portion, used to lock in the change while the body is still responsive. Across the session, the same recovery your body already knows how to do, with the precision to make it last.

Why it works

Thirty minutes of focused hands-on work. Every session.

The first half of every visit is hands-on. The clinician reads tissue and joint mechanics by touch, which is information no scan and no exercise can produce, and treats them at the same time. The nervous system responds to manual input through documented pathways: pain inhibition, autonomic regulation, mobility restoration. This is the part of the treatment that does the most physiological work.

The same clinician sees you every visit, so each session continues where the last one ended. The body remembers the work, and the work compounds. Movement and exercise follow the hands, used to reinforce what was just changed and rehearse the pattern you want the body to keep.

Questions

  • Is manual therapy the same as massage?

    Not exactly. Massage therapy focuses on muscle relaxation and circulation. Manual therapy in physical therapy is clinical, it addresses joint mechanics, muscle length, motor patterns, and tissue restrictions in the context of a diagnosis. The goal isn't relaxation (though that often follows); it's restoring movement and reducing the structural factors driving your symptoms.

  • Is it painful?

    It shouldn't be. There's often a pressure or stretch sensation (sometimes described as a 'productive discomfort') but pain is information, not a goal. Clinicians calibrate in real time based on your response. If something doesn't feel right, say so.

  • How much hands-on time will I actually get?

    About thirty minutes of direct, uninterrupted hands-on work in every session. The exercise and movement portion follows from what the manual work changed, which makes the movement more effective and the outcomes more durable.

  • Does insurance cover it?

    Manual therapy is a billable PT service and is covered by most insurance plans when PT is covered. We accept select plans. For out-of-network PPOs, partial reimbursement after the deductible is common. A superbill is provided after every visit.
